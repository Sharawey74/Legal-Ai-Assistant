# Non-Compete Clauses — California

## 1. Legal Framework
California has a strong public policy favoring employee mobility. Under **California Business and Professions Code Section 16600**, "every contract by which anyone is restrained from engaging in a lawful profession, trade, or business of any kind is to that extent void." 

## 2. Enforceability for Tech Employees
Under California Business and Professions Code Section 16600, a non-compete clause is generally unenforceable for tech employees, regardless of the narrowness of the restriction or the tech employee's level of compensation. The only exceptions are statutory (e.g., the sale of a business or dissolution of a partnership).

## 3. Edwards v. Arthur Andersen LLP (2008)
In this landmark ruling, the California Supreme Court firmly rejected the "narrow restraint" exception to Section 16600. The court clarified that non-compete agreements are strictly prohibited in California for standard employment contracts, invalidating employer attempts to prevent departing employees from working for competitors.

## 4. Drafting Alternatives
Since traditional non-competes are void, employers in California protect their interests by utilizing:
- **Confidentiality and Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs)**: To prevent tech employees from taking proprietary code or business strategies.
- **Trade Secret Protections**: Relying on the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA).
- **Employee Non-Solicitation Clauses**: Though their enforceability has also been increasingly scrutinized following *AMN Healthcare, Inc. v. Aya Healthcare Services, Inc. (2018)*.
