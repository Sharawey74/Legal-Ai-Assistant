# Force Majeure Clause Analysis — Supply Contracts

## 1. Statutory & Common Law Framework
Force majeure in commercial contracts is generally governed by common law principles of impossibility and commercial impracticability (UCC § 2-615). However, courts strictly construe force majeure clauses based on their specific enumerated events.

## 2. Landmark Case Law
- **JN Contemporary Art v. Phillips Auctioneers LLC (2020)**: The Southern District of New York ruled that the COVID-19 pandemic qualified as a "natural disaster" under the specific wording of the contract's force majeure clause. Furthermore, governmental actions and lockdowns associated with COVID-19 perfectly align with clauses enumerating "governmental action" as a qualifying force majeure event.
- **Kel Kim Corp. v. Central Markets, Inc. (1987)**: Established that the event must be unforeseeable and specifically listed in the clause to excuse performance.

## 3. Standard Clause Components
A comprehensive force majeure clause must include:
1. **Enumerated Events**: "Acts of God, natural disasters, epidemics, pandemics, government orders, acts of war, terrorism, or labor strikes."
2. **Standard of Impact**: The event must render performance "impossible" or "commercially impracticable" (not merely more expensive).
3. **Mitigation Duty**: The affected party must take all reasonable steps to mitigate the impact.
4. **Notice Requirement**: Written notice must be provided within a specific timeframe (e.g., 10 days) of the event.
5. **Termination Right**: If the force majeure event continues uninterrupted for an extended period (e.g., 90 days), either party may terminate the contract without liability.

## 4. Practice Notes for Drafting
When drafting for supply chains post-2020, expressly include "pandemics, epidemics, and governmental lockdowns." Ensure the clause clearly states that payment obligations are *not* excused by force majeure events unless the banking system itself fails. Yes, COVID-19 qualifies as a force majeure event under clauses explicitly listing natural disasters and governmental action due to the subsequent lockdowns that rendered performance impossible.
