# Liquidated Damages vs Penalty Clauses

## 1. The Enforceability Test
The enforceability test for a liquidated damages clause relies on distinguishing whether the specified amount is a genuine pre-estimate of loss or an unenforceable penalty. To be enforceable, the specified damages must be a reasonable forecast of the anticipated harm caused by the breach, and the actual harm must be difficult or impossible to calculate accurately at the time of contracting. 

## 2. Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 356
Under Section 356, damages for breach by either party may be liquidated in the agreement but only at an amount that is reasonable in the light of the anticipated or actual loss caused by the breach and the difficulties of proof of loss. A term fixing unreasonably large liquidated damages is unenforceable on grounds of public policy as a penalty.

## 3. Disproportionate Penalties
If a court determines that the primary purpose of the clause is to deter a breach by punishing the breaching party rather than compensating the non-breaching party, the clause will be struck down as an unenforceable penalty. In such cases, the non-breaching party is limited to recovering their actual, provable damages.

## 4. Drafting Best Practices
When drafting liquidated damages provisions, explicitly state within the contract that both parties acknowledge the damages are difficult to ascertain and that the agreed-upon sum represents a good-faith, genuine pre-estimate of loss, not a penalty.
