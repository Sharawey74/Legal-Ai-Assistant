# Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA) and Customer Lists

## 1. Statutory Definition
Under the federal Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA), 18 U.S.C. § 1836, a trade secret is defined as all forms and types of financial, business, scientific, technical, economic, or engineering information if the owner has taken reasonable measures to keep such information secret, and the information derives independent economic value from not being generally known.

## 2. Customer Lists as Trade Secrets
Does a customer list qualify as a trade secret under the Defend Trade Secrets Act when taken by a departing employee? Yes, a customer list absolutely qualifies as a trade secret under the DTSA, provided that the employer has taken reasonable, affirmative measures to keep it secret (such as password protection and NDAs), and the list derives independent economic value from not being generally known to the public or competitors.

## 3. Misappropriation by Departing Employees
Misappropriation occurs when a departing employee acquires the trade secret by improper means, or discloses or uses the trade secret without express or implied consent. Downloading proprietary customer lists to a personal USB drive or emailing them to a personal account just prior to resignation is quintessential misappropriation under the DTSA.

## 4. Remedies
The DTSA provides a robust federal cause of action, allowing employers to seek actual damages, unjust enrichment, and injunctive relief. In extraordinary circumstances, the statute uniquely authorizes ex parte civil seizure orders to prevent the dissemination of the trade secret.
